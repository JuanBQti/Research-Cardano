# Analysis of Cardano improvement proposal CIP-0023

---

## Executive summary

This report evaluates Cardano improvement proposal 0023 (CIP-0023), ‘Fair Min Fees’, against the foundational design principles of the Shelley delegation specification (SL-D1) and empirical findings from the ‘Analysis of Cardano’s Incentive Mechanism’ (‘the incentives report’). 

Adopting a skeptical lens, we assess whether CIP-0023 justifies altering a system that has demonstrated remarkable stability. Currently, the network sustains approximately 3,000 registered pools and around 1,800 active contributors. CIP-0023 proposes a new protocol parameter, `minPoolMargin`, to enforce a minimum variable fee. 

This analysis finds that the proposal lacks rigorous game-theoretic modeling of long-term equilibria. Our simulations show that higher margins sustain more pools (e.g. 1,725 viable at 5% margin vs 100 at 0%), but this simultaneously reduces delegator rewards. The proposal needs further modeling to meet the high bar required for implementation; the risks identified outweigh the unsubstantiated benefits.

---

## Merits of the proposal

### Proposed changes
CIP-0023 introduces `minPoolMargin`, establishing a lower bound on the variable fee (margin) stake pool operators (SPOs) can charge. 
* **Mechanism:** To maintain backward compatibility, the parameter is initialized at 0. Enforcement occurs during reward calculation via ‘clamping’: if a pool’s margin is below `minPoolMargin`, the protocol overrides it with the minimum value.
* **Scope:** No changes are made to the existing `minPoolCost` (minimum fixed fee, currently 170 ADA). 
* **Formula:** The operator’s take is calculated as:
  $$operator\_take = max(minPoolMargin, registered\_margin) * (pot - fixed) + fixed$$

### Problems addressed from the incentives report
The proposal relates to the ‘Viability Gap’—the struggle for small pools to cover operational costs (currently at ~250 ADA/epoch given current ADA/USD exchange rates) when fixed fees dilute returns at low stake levels. Our simulations (2,000 initial pools, Pareto shape b=2.0, 150 epochs) yield the following insights:

| Scenario | Margin | Fixed fee logic | Viable pools | Key observation |
| :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- | :--- |
| **Collapse** | 0% | Strict (170 ADA) | 100 | Mass retirements (1,900). 95% of the network exits. |
| **Baseline** | 2% | Strict (170 ADA) | 779 | High churn; 1,221 retirements despite the low margin floor. |
| **Sustainability** | 5% | Strict (170 ADA) | 1,725 | Significant improvement in viability (275 retirements). |
| **Adaptive** | 5% | Adaptive (>=170) | 1,998 | Survival nearly total, but at the cost of delegator ROS. |
| **Over-protection**| 10% | Strict (170 ADA) | 1,998 | Almost total survival; two retirements due to extreme top-heaviness. |
| **Adaptive-high** | 10% | Adaptive (>=170) | 2,000 | Absolute survival; likely incentivizes multi-pool operator (MPO) fragmentation. |
| **Zero-fixed** | 5% | minPoolCost=0 | 302 | Eliminating fixed fees results in 1,698 retirements. |

**Note on simulation logic:** * **Strict:** Models a hyper-competitive environment where all operators set their fixed fee strictly to the protocol minimum (170 ADA). This simulates a ‘price war’ where operators refuse to raise costs for fear of losing delegators to lower-fee competitors.
* **Adaptive:** Models a survivalist environment where pools dynamically raise their fixed fees above the minimum to cover actual operational overhead (~250 ADA). While this ensures pool solvency, it reduces the return on stake (ROS), triggering the migration of rational delegators.

**Deductions from data:**
1.  **Top-heavy resilience:** With a more concentrated initial distribution, the ‘Collapse’ scenario is severe, with only 100 pools surviving at 0% margin and strict competition at `minPoolCost`. This highlights how vulnerable the current tail of the distribution is to fee-war dynamics.
2.  **Fixed fee necessity:** The simulation with `minPoolCost=0` confirms that the fixed fee is a critical protection for decentralization. Without it, the ‘scale barrier’ is insurmountable for approximately 85% of the initial pool population.
3.  **The scale barrier:** Small pools (e.g. 1M ADA stake) cannot generate enough income from a 5% margin alone (~12.5 ADA) to cover server costs (~250 ADA). A pool requires roughly 15.5M ADA in stake to break even without a fixed fee, leading to 1,698 retirements in the simulation. It is positive that the proposal explicitly avoids modifying or removing `minPoolCost`.
4.  **The migration feedback loop:** In ‘adaptive’ scenarios, pools that raise fixed costs to survive see their ROS drop, leading to a 2% per epoch stake outflow. This creates a slow-motion abandonment of pools that over-correct for their costs.

## Challenges and risks

### Trade-offs and game theory
The primary trade-off is operator viability versus delegator returns. While the mechanism stabilizes a larger number of pools, this stability comes at the cost of delegator returns and could potentially lead to more MPOs.
* **MPO advantage:** Large operators can fragment into multiple pools to capture the `minPoolMargin` across a wider stake base while sharing some relay infrastructure. 
* **Plausibility:** The mechanism holds if migration is slow, but assumes no collusion. If delegators become apathetic due to lower overall ROS, the Nakamoto coefficient could drop.

### Security and system gaming

1.  **Sybil spoofing:** Clamping allows ‘pre-hike’ spoofing where an adversary advertises a low margin to attract stake, knowing the protocol will move it to the minimum, siphoning rewards from unsuspecting delegators.

---

## High bar for implementation

The current Cardano incentive model, while imperfect, is functional. The incentives report shows an equilibrium of 1,500 to 2,000 active pools with low retirement rates post-2023. CIP-0023 fails to provide:
* **Formal verification:** No proof that the clamping mechanism is immune to adversarial manipulation.
* **Superiority:** It does not demonstrate a clear 20% gain in decentralization metrics over the status quo.

## Conclusions and next steps

CIP-0023 has merits but requires additional modeling and further simulations to identify if there are specific combinations of `minMargins` and `minPoolCosts` where it can be beneficial for both operators and delegators. The simulations we have performed show that the introduction of `minMargin` does improve the viability for many pools, but this comes at the cost of diminished rewards for delegators. Simulations also show that the removal and, in some cases, even the reduction of the `minPoolCost` parameter would paradoxically lead to centralization and create greater imbalances for small operators, rather than fostering a more competitive marketplace.
