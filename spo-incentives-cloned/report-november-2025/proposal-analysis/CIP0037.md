# Analysis of CIP-0037: Evaluating the impact of dynamic saturation on the Cardano ecosystem

## Executive summary

CIP-0037 proposes a fundamental shift in Cardano's incentive mechanism by making a stake pool’s saturation point a direct function of its pledge. This analysis explores both the strategic merits of the proposal—primarily its ability to align capital commitment with network influence—and the systemic risks associated with a capital-constrained operator landscape.

The proposal introduces a scalable ‘skin-in-the-game’ requirement that remains negligible for small pools while becoming a critical constraint for large-scale operations. While this effectively targets Multi-Pool Operations (MPOs), it also introduces a potential ‘growth ceiling’ for community pools. This report evaluates these trade-offs to determine if the proposal achieves its goals without compromising the network's inclusive ethos.

## Strategic benefits and incentive alignment

### Deferred capital burden for small pools

A key merit of CIP-0037 is that it does not impose an immediate financial burden on new or small operators. For a median pool with 1,000 ADA pledge:
* **Current status:** Most pools in this tier hold less than 1M ADA in stake.
* **Proposed ceiling:** Even with a conservative 10% lower-limit floor, the pool’s saturation point would be approximately 7.6M ADA.
* **Incentive timing:** The operator is not required to provide more pledge until they have successfully attracted over 7.6M ADA, at which point the pool is likely generating enough revenue to facilitate a higher pledge.

### Targeted mitigation of MPO leverage

The proposal directly addresses the ‘influence-without-capital’ loophole. By tying saturation to pledge, an MPO attempting to run 10 pools would need 10 times the pledge to keep all pools fully saturated. This forces a choice: consolidate into a single high-pledge pool or split the pledge in multiple pools with lower saturation thresholds.

### Calculating the saturation floor for median operators (1,000 ADA)

Under current conditions ($k=500$), the standard saturation point is 76M ADA. For the primary cohort of 1,969 pools with a median pledge of 1,000 ADA:
* **Proportional saturation:** 1,000 ADA pledge / 500,000 ADA benchmark = 0.2% saturation capacity, roughtly 152K ADA. 
* **Effective capacity:** The ‘lower limit’ floor (10% to 20%) overrides the raw calculation, granting a capacity of 7.6M to 15.2M ADA.

### Impact on the 30M - 50M ADA tier (10,000 ADA pledge)

A more complex scenario emerges for established pools in the 30M - 50M ADA tier, where the median pledge is 10,000 ADA. For these 142 pools, the transition to CIP-0037 would be disruptive:
* **Raw proportionality:** 10,000 ADA pledge / 500,000 ADA benchmark = 2% saturation capacity (1.52M ADA).
* **Forced oversaturation:** Even with a 20% floor (15.2M ADA), a pool currently holding 40M ADA would immediately become **263% oversaturated**.
* **The "reward penalty" logic:** Because rewards are capped at the saturation point, delegators in these pools would see their rewards slashed by more than half unless the operator increases their pledge to 40,000 ADA.

## Systemic risks and practical consequences

### Pool performance should be more important than capital in it's ability to grow

If pledge becomes the primary driver of growth capacity, the network risks pivoting away from meritocracy toward a system that favors existing wealth. In a decentralized ecosystem, operational excellence, security contributions, and community trust should define a pool's success. Pledge influence a0, was originally thought only to avoid sybil behavior, but not necesarilly as a primary factor of pool diferentiation. 

### Risk of opaque centralization

As operators approach their pledge-based saturation ceiling, the pressure to increase pledge may lead to ‘pledge-as-a-service’ markets. If operators ‘rent’ capital from off-chain entities to increase their saturation limit, the ‘skin-in-the-game’ signal is diluted. This could centralize real power in the hands of invisible capital providers, creating a layer of centralization that is not visible on-chain. This is something that can still happen without CIP0037, so this risk is not intrinsic to the proposal. 

## Conclusion and recommendations

CIP-0037 is a powerful tool for aligning economic commitment with protocol influence. Its primary strength lies in its scalability; it leaves small pools unaffected in their early stages while forcing a ‘pay-to-play’ model on those who gain massive influence.
